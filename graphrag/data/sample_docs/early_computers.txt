The Dawn of the Digital Age: Early Computers and the Pioneers Who Built Them

The history of electronic computing spans a remarkable decade from the mid-1940s to the mid-1950s, during which a handful of machines and the engineers behind them transformed humanity's relationship with information. These early computers — room-sized machines of glowing vacuum tubes, clicking relays, and punched paper tape — were not merely faster calculators. They were the first demonstrations that a universal machine for information processing was physically realizable, laying the groundwork for every computer, smartphone, and microchip that followed.

The most famous early computer was ENIAC — the Electronic Numerical Integrator and Computer — built at the University of Pennsylvania by J. Presper Eckert and John Mauchly. ENIAC was completed in 1945 and officially unveiled in February 1946. It was enormous: 30 tons, 18,000 vacuum tubes, 70,000 resistors, 10,000 capacitors, filling a room 50 feet long and 30 feet wide. It consumed 150 kilowatts of power and performed approximately 5,000 additions per second — faster than any previous computing device. ENIAC was initially classified and used primarily for computing artillery firing tables for the US Army's Ballistic Research Laboratory. It was later used for calculations related to the hydrogen bomb. ENIAC was not a stored-program computer; its programs were entered by physically setting switches and plugging cables. Nevertheless, ENIAC proved that large-scale electronic computing was feasible and ignited the post-war race to build more capable machines.

Eckert and Mauchly's subsequent project, UNIVAC I (Universal Automatic Computer), became the first commercially available general-purpose computer in the United States. Delivered to the US Census Bureau in 1951, UNIVAC I was notable for using magnetic tape for input and output rather than punched cards, and it was designed from the outset for business data processing rather than scientific calculation. UNIVAC achieved public fame when it was used by CBS television to predict the outcome of the 1952 US presidential election based on early returns — correctly predicting Dwight Eisenhower's landslide victory, though CBS initially refused to broadcast the prediction for fear of embarrassing inaccuracy. UNIVAC's accuracy on election night made "UNIVAC" temporarily synonymous with "computer" in the American public imagination.

In Britain, a separate lineage of early computing developed from wartime codebreaking. The most significant wartime machine was Colossus, built at Bletchley Park beginning in 1943. Colossus was designed by Tommy Flowers, a Post Office engineer, to break the Lorenz cipher used by the German high command — a more sophisticated cipher than the Enigma. Colossus was the world's first programmable, electronic digital computer, using 1,500 vacuum tubes and paper tape input to search for cipher wheel settings at electronic speed. Alan Turing and his colleagues at Bletchley Park worked alongside Colossus, developing the cryptanalytic methods the machine implemented. Ten Colossus machines were built; they played a decisive role in Allied victory by enabling decryption of high-level German strategic communications. Because of wartime secrecy, Colossus's existence was not officially acknowledged until the 1970s, depriving its builders of the recognition they deserved and distorting the historical record of who built the first electronic computer.

The first operational stored-program computer — the machine that embodied John von Neumann's and Alan Turing's theoretical architectural vision in working hardware — was the Manchester Baby, formally known as the Small-Scale Experimental Machine (SSEM). Built at the University of Manchester by Freddie Williams and Tom Kilburn, the Baby ran its first program on June 21, 1948. The machine used the Williams-Kilburn tube — a cathode ray tube adapted to store binary data — as its memory, solving one of the key engineering challenges of the stored-program concept. The Baby's first program, written by Kilburn, searched for the highest factor of a number by trial division and ran for 52 minutes, producing the correct result. The Manchester Baby was quickly followed by the full Manchester Mark 1, operational in 1949, and by the Ferranti Mark 1, the first commercially produced stored-program computer, delivered to the University of Manchester in 1951.

A parallel achievement was the EDSAC (Electronic Delay Storage Automatic Calculator), built at Cambridge University by Maurice Wilkes and his team. EDSAC ran its first program on May 6, 1949, just months after the Manchester Baby, and is widely credited as the first practical stored-program computer used for real scientific work. Wilkes also developed the concept of microprogramming — storing the control logic of a processor as a set of instructions in a small, fast memory — a technique that became standard in processor design for decades.

Grace Hopper was one of the most influential figures in early computing, particularly in making computers accessible to non-specialists. A mathematician and US Navy officer, Hopper worked on the Harvard Mark I and Mark II computers during the war and joined Eckert-Mauchly Computer Corporation, where she worked on UNIVAC. In 1952, Hopper created the first compiler — a program that translated high-level symbolic code into machine instructions — called the A-0 system. This was a conceptual revolution: it demonstrated that programmers did not need to write in binary or assembly language to control a computer. Hopper's work eventually led to the development of COBOL (Common Business-Oriented Language), a programming language she championed that became one of the most widely used languages in business data processing, with hundreds of billions of lines of COBOL code estimated to still be in active use today. Hopper is also credited with coining the term "bug" in its computing sense, after her team found an actual moth causing a malfunction in the Harvard Mark II.

The transition from vacuum tubes to transistors in the late 1950s represented a fundamental technological discontinuity. Vacuum tubes were bulky, hot, fragile, power-hungry, and had limited lifetimes. The transistor — invented at Bell Labs by Bardeen, Brattain, and Shockley in 1947 — was smaller, faster, cooler, more reliable, and far cheaper. IBM's 7090, introduced in 1959 as a fully transistorized successor to the vacuum-tube 709, was dramatically faster and more reliable than its predecessor. The IBM 1401, a transistorized business computer introduced in 1959, became one of the most widely installed computers of the early 1960s. The transition to transistors made computing economically accessible to a much wider range of organizations, setting the stage for the minicomputer revolution of the 1960s and the personal computer revolution of the 1970s that followed. The architectural ideas pioneered by Eckert, Mauchly, Wilkes, von Neumann, and Turing — stored programs, sequential instruction execution, separate memory and processing units — remained essentially unchanged as the technology scaled from vacuum tubes to transistors to integrated circuits to billion-transistor microprocessors.
